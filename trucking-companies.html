How this list was built

We rank the largest trucking companies by number of power units (tractors) in each state's FMCSA registration. We then enrich each carrier using four FMCSA datasets:

  • Cargo type — pulled from the carrier's MCS-150 commodity flags (Company Census File).
  • Insurance company — the most recent active BIPD policy on file (ActPendInsur).
  • Safety Rating — the carrier's official DOT safety rating (Satisfactory, Conditional, Unsatisfactory, or Not Rated).
  • Crashes (24 months) — federally recordable crashes from the national Crash File since January 2024, including fatalities and injuries.
Patterns in the data

What stands out across the 50-state directory

When you look at every active carrier — not just the biggest — a handful of patterns jump out of the FMCSA data:

1
Alaska runs on construction, not freight. It's the only state where Construction outranks General Freight as the most-hauled cargo. Makes sense — long winters, heavy infrastructure work, fewer long-haul dry-van lanes.
2
The Corn Belt is exactly what you'd expect. Iowa, Nebraska, and South Dakota all default to Grain, Feed & Hay as the top commodity. Ag freight still dominates those three state fleets.
3
Maine and Vermont lean heavy and industrial. Their top cargo categories are Construction and Machinery & Large Objects rather than General Freight — a fingerprint of smaller, specialty fleets vs. long-haul dry van.
4
Hawaii is its own insurance market. Island Insurance Company Ltd is the top insurer for major Hawaiian fleets — a local carrier you won't find dominating anywhere else.
5
Michigan belongs to Cherokee. Cherokee Insurance Company is the top insurer of major Michigan fleets — a surprisingly niche choice for such a large commercial-trucking state.
6
ACE American is the national heavyweight. It's the #1 insurer for major fleets in Arizona, Georgia, Illinois, New Jersey, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Tennessee, and Texas — more states than any other carrier on the list.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is "largest" defined here?

By number of tractors (power units) reported to the FMCSA on each carrier's most recent MCS-150 filing. That's the same filing carriers use to register and renew their operating authority, so it's the most consistent apples-to-apples measure of fleet size across states.

Where does the insurance data come from?

From FMCSA's ActPendInsur (Active or Pending Insurance) file. We show the most recent active BIPD (bodily injury and property damage) policy per carrier, along with the liability limit when disclosed. Carriers in every state must carry at least $750K to $5M in BIPD coverage depending on what they haul.

What's a "recordable crash"?

FMCSA counts a crash as recordable if a tow-away was required, anyone needed medical treatment away from the scene, or someone was killed. The crash counts here cover roughly the last 24 months from the national Crash File. A higher number is not automatically bad — large fleets run far more miles and have more exposure — but comparing crashes to fleet size is a useful safety signal.

Why do some carriers show "Not Rated"?

DOT only issues a Safety Rating after a compliance review (a full on-site audit). Most carriers — even large ones — have never been through one, so their rating is blank. "Not Rated" does not mean unsafe; it means no formal review has been performed.

How often is this data updated?

The FMCSA datasets we pull from are refreshed daily on data.transportation.gov. We re-sync this directory monthly. The last refresh date is shown in the byline.

I'm a carrier — how do I get listed or correct my info?

Every row on this page comes straight from FMCSA. If a cargo type, insurance company, or address looks wrong, update your MCS-150 or have your insurance agent refile. Once it's corrected at the FMCSA it will flow into our next monthly refresh.
